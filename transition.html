Transitioning to
Open Infrastructure

The path from proprietary to commons-based infrastructure is achieved through strategic changes in licensing and governance. Licensing is the key mechanism that defines access, control, and openness.

01

From Proprietary to Shared

In proprietary systems, licenses restrict use, modification, and redistribution, keeping infrastructure closed and centrally controlled. The model prioritizes profit extraction over community benefit. As a result, development is siloed, innovation is limited to a single company, and the infrastructure remains vulnerable to corporate decisions.

Open-source licenses work differently. They grant broad rights to use, study, modify, and share resources, effectively transforming closed systems into shared infrastructure. When organizations transition—like Ingres did in 2004—they replace exclusive licensing with frameworks allowing external developers to access and contribute. This shift enables distributed development, community innovation, and sustainable infrastructure.

02

Understanding Open Licenses

The type of open license determines how "common" the infrastructure remains. Different licenses balance innovation with sustainability in different ways:

Copyleft Licenses

(GPL, AGPL)

Require that derivative works remain open-source. Prevent re-privatization and ensure the commons stays protected.

Permissive Licenses

(MIT, Apache 2.0)

Allow reuse without requiring derived works to remain open. Maximize adoption but offer less protection.

Source-Available

(BSL, SSPL)

Middle ground between proprietary and open. Source is visible but restrictions apply under certain conditions.

Public Domain

(CC0, Unlicense)

Maximum freedom. No restrictions on use, allowing complete freedom but offering no reciprocal obligations.

03

Licensing as Governance

Licensing functions as a governance mechanism because it structures participation, defines rights and responsibilities, and enables digital infrastructures to operate as collectively maintained resources rather than privately controlled systems. When choosing a license, organizations fundamentally shape:

Who Can Participate

Permissive licenses welcome everyone. Copyleft licenses welcome contributors but require reciprocity. Restrictive licenses limit participation by design.

How Knowledge Flows

Open licenses enable knowledge sharing. Copyleft ensures improvements flow back. Proprietary licenses create silos where knowledge remains captured.

Long-term Sustainability

Commons thrive when reciprocity is built-in. Copyleft ensures this. Permissive licenses trust goodwill. Proprietary models depend on corporate survival.

Commercial Viability

Open licenses don't prevent commercial use. Many companies build profitable services around open-source software while maintaining the commons.

04

Transitioning Your Infrastructure

If you're considering transitioning proprietary infrastructure to open-source, licensing is your first decision. Consider:

1. Your Values

Do you want to ensure derivative works remain open? Copyleft is for you. Prefer maximum freedom? Choose permissive.

2. Your Goals

Do you want maximum adoption? Permissive licenses win. Seeking long-term sustainability? Copyleft builds reciprocal responsibility.

3. Your Timeline

Early adoption matters. Choose a license aligned with your long-term vision and commit to it consistently.
