How the Simulation Works

This agent-based model tests a fundamental question in cooperation theory: what incentive level is sufficient to encourage rational actors to shift from free-riding to contributing? The simulation is grounded in real data and economic theory.

Step 1 — Agents

Each agent represents a small firm. At every simulation tick, it decides whether to contribute to or free-ride on the digital commons based on its decision rule.

Contribute if:
Benefit(incentive) + Reputation > Cost


Free-ride if:
Benefit(free_ride) > Penalty

Step 2 — Calibration

Decision rule weights are derived from empirical GitHub contribution data. Agent behavior reflects real open-source patterns observed in practice. The Contribution Gini index informs the starting distribution of contributors vs. free-riders.

Step 3 — Threshold Discovery

The simulation sweeps across incentive levels, running multiple parameter settings to identify the minimum incentive at which the majority rationally switch from free-riding to contributing. Rooted in Olson (1971): selective incentives drive cooperation.

Policy Implications

This simulation demonstrates that free-riding is rational for most actors without sufficient incentives. However, governments and organizations can influence these incentives through:

Tax Incentives

Tax credits for companies that contribute to open-source projects make contributing more economically rational.

Public Procurement

Government contracts favoring companies with strong open-source contributions create reputational incentives.

Funding Programs

Direct funding to open-source projects increases the benefits of contributing and reduces the cost.

Community Recognition

Visibility and reputation within the open-source community matter. Public recognition amplifies intrinsic motivations.
